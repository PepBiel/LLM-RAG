# Tomato yellowing leaves field check

Yellowing leaves in tomato can have multiple causes, and the first useful action is to describe the pattern. Lower-leaf yellowing can be associated with water stress, nitrogen deficiency, natural leaf aging, root constraints, salinity, or disease. Yellowing between leaf veins may suggest nutrient imbalance. Yellowing with spots, lesions, or wilting may require disease or pest investigation. A reliable recommendation needs crop stage, symptom distribution, irrigation status, and recent operations.

If yellowing appears near one irrigation line or in a repeated row pattern, inspect water distribution before assuming a nutrient or pest cause. Check whether affected plants are in a drier part of the wetting pattern, whether emitters are blocked, and whether the latest irrigation reached the expected depth. If yellowing is uniform across the plot, review fertility program, recent weather, and root-zone conditions.

A practical triage sequence is: inspect irrigation delivery, compare affected and unaffected plants, check soil moisture at representative points, look for root health issues, review recent fertilizer events, then scout for insects and disease signs. The operator should avoid making a single-cause diagnosis from one symptom.

For a decision-support API, the answer should produce a prioritized checklist. It should say which hypothesis is most consistent with the available data, what evidence would confirm it, and what action is safe to take immediately.
