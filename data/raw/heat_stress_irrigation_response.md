# Heat stress and irrigation response protocol

Heat stress risk increases when air temperature rises, humidity is low, wind is present, and soil water is limited. A crop under heat stress can lose water faster than expected, especially during midday. However, increasing irrigation without checking soil and system conditions can create other problems. The better operational response is to combine weather context, soil moisture trend, crop stage, and field symptoms.

When heat risk and low soil moisture occur together, prioritize field inspection and irrigation system verification. Confirm that the next irrigation event is scheduled, that the system can deliver enough water, and that the wetting pattern reaches active roots. For drip systems, short and frequent cycles may sometimes reduce stress in shallow root zones, but the correct program depends on soil texture and system design.

Heat-related symptoms can overlap with water stress, nutrient issues, and disease. Leaf curling, temporary wilting, and slowed growth can be stress responses. If the crop recovers in the evening and soil moisture is adequate, monitor. If symptoms persist overnight or progress, investigate root-zone constraints, disease, salinity, or severe water deficit.

Weather data services such as NASA POWER can support context by providing temperature, humidity, radiation, wind, and precipitation variables. In an MVP assistant, simulated sensor data can stand in for live IoT telemetry. In a later product phase, the API could fetch real weather data for a plot location and use it to enrich the diagnosis.
