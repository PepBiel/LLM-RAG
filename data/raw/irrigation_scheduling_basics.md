# Irrigation scheduling basics for field operations

Irrigation scheduling is the operational process of deciding when to irrigate, how long to irrigate, and what field checks are needed before changing a program. A good decision should combine soil conditions, crop stage, recent weather, irrigation system capacity, and field observations. A sensor value alone is not enough: operators should compare the reading with the expected root-zone behavior, the soil texture, and the last irrigation event.

For sandy or sandy-loam soils, water can move quickly below the active root zone. A low soil moisture reading in this context should trigger a check of irrigation frequency and emitter function before simply increasing the duration of a cycle. For clay or clay-loam soils, the response can be slower, and over-irrigation may create waterlogging or disease pressure. The practical goal is to maintain enough available water for crop demand while avoiding losses below the root zone.

Operational checks should follow a sequence. First, confirm that the sensor is working and installed at a representative depth. Second, compare the latest value with trend data rather than a single measurement. Third, inspect wetting pattern, pressure, filtration, valves, and emitter uniformity. Fourth, connect the decision to crop symptoms. Wilting, leaf curling, poor growth, and lower-leaf yellowing can be related to water stress, but they can also be caused by nutrition, root disease, salinity, or pests.

When a plot has low soil moisture and visible symptoms, the safest first recommendation is not a chemical action. The first step is to verify water delivery and uniformity. If water delivery is normal, the next checks are nutrient status, root health, salinity, and pest scouting. A decision-support assistant should make this uncertainty explicit and prioritize low-risk field verification.
