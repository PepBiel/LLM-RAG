# Drip irrigation troubleshooting checklist

Drip irrigation problems often appear as crop stress in lines, blocks, or zones. Before changing the agronomic plan, operators should inspect the hydraulic system. The core checks are pressure at the head unit, pressure at the lateral end, filter condition, valve status, leaks, blocked emitters, damaged tape, and whether the irrigation event actually ran for the expected duration.

Low soil moisture in a drip-irrigated vegetable plot can be caused by insufficient runtime, excessive interval between cycles, clogged emitters, poor pressure regulation, a closed valve, controller error, or a mismatch between crop demand and irrigation design. If the problem appears in specific rows, emitter or line issues are more likely. If the whole block is dry, schedule, pump, filtration, or supply constraints become more likely.

A useful field workflow is: confirm controller logs, inspect the head unit, flush laterals if needed, compare wetting pattern across representative rows, and record before/after sensor readings. When crop symptoms are visible, document whether symptoms align with the irrigation layout. This is important because a symptom pattern can distinguish irrigation delivery problems from nutrient or pest problems.

For tomatoes under drip irrigation, low root-zone moisture plus yellowing lower leaves should trigger a water-delivery check first. If moisture recovers and symptoms stop progressing, continue monitoring. If symptoms progress despite corrected moisture, investigate nitrogen availability, root disease, salinity, and pest presence.
